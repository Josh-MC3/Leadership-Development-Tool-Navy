The Leadership
Jacket

A working record you start as a Midshipman or officer candidate and keep filling in as you commission, qualify, and promote. No one grades this. No one screens it. It exists because the most important leadership project you'll ever run is the one nobody assigns you.

How this works

This isn't a one-sitting form. It's built around six phases, each anchored to a book and a set of reflections. Read the book, sit with the questions, write honestly — not for an audience. Your answers save automatically in this browser. When you're ready, export the whole jacket as a document you can keep, print, or carry forward to your next command.

There's a question underneath all six phases:

If no promotion board, fitrep ranking, command screen, or title could see your answer — who would you choose to become anyway?
Phase I — Identity & Foundations
Who am I beneath the rank?

The Leaders Who Shaped You

Begin by looking outward before you look inward.
Not started
The Road to Character
David Brooks
Draws the line between résumé virtues (what gets you promoted) and eulogy virtues (what gets remembered). Read this before you write anything else here.
Think of the best leader you've served under. What specifically made them effective — and how did they make people feel?
Think of the worst leader you've served under. What damaged trust, morale, or performance — and which of their tendencies do you recognize as a trap for yourself?
Which "résumé virtues" have driven your career so far? Which "eulogy virtues" do you most admire in others — and where are the two in tension for you right now?
Why this exercise first
Every later phase assumes you've named your influences honestly. Skipping ahead to "vision" before doing this work tends to produce a leadership philosophy borrowed from a fitrep rather than earned from experience.

Phase II — Leadership in Practice
How do I want to lead?

Ownership and Trust

Where authority actually comes from.
Not started
Turn the Ship Around!
L. David Marquet
A submarine captain's account of shifting from leader-follower to leader-leader. The most practically naval book on this list.
Good to Great
Jim Collins
Not written for the military, but its core finding — that enduring performance comes from disciplined people, thought, and action, not charisma — translates directly to command climate.
Where do you currently retain control that you could hand to the people who actually do the work? What's stopping you?
Collins distinguishes "Level 5 leaders" — quietly determined, ambitious for the mission rather than themselves — from charismatic ones. Which type have you served under, and which are you becoming?

How do I want to lead?

People, Plainly

Influence without rank — and influence with it.
Not started
How to Win Friends and Influence People
Dale Carnegie
Unfashionable to recommend, durable in practice. Most of what it teaches is just paying genuine attention to people — which gets harder, not easier, the more rank you hold.
Crucial Conversations
Grenny, Patterson, McMillan, Switzler, Gregory
A framework for the conversations everyone avoids — high stakes, strong emotion, differing opinions. Required reading before you ever have to deliver a counseling, a hard fitrep conversation, or tell a senior something they don't want to hear.
Think of a conversation you've been avoiding — with a senior, a peer, or someone you lead. What's actually at stake, and what's stopped you from having it?
Carnegie's central claim is that people respond to genuine interest and respect far more than to argument. Where in your leadership do you reach for authority before you reach for understanding?

Phase III — The Weight of Command
What happens when the stakes are real?

Risk, Ownership, and Its Limits

Where accountability ends and martyrdom begins.
Not started
It's Your Ship
Captain D. Michael Abrashoff
The classic. Read it for the ideas, and read it skeptically — ask what's specific to one ship and one moment in time, versus what's genuinely transferable.
Extreme Ownership
Jocko Willink & Leif Babin
Worth engaging critically rather than reverently — total ownership is a useful corrective, but it has a failure mode too.
Abrashoff took real risks to change his ship's culture. Would you have had the courage to take the same risks — and what's actually held you back, when you didn't?
Where does "extreme ownership" become unhealthy for you specifically? What burdens are actually yours to carry, and which ones have you taken on that aren't?

Phase IV — Judgment & Decision-Making
How do good leaders think?

Bias, Confidence, and Uncertainty

The thinking behind the decisions.
Not started
Thinking, Fast and Slow
Daniel Kahneman
The map of your own cognitive blind spots. Read it as a user manual for the brain you'll be making decisions with under pressure.
The Black Swan
Nassim Nicholas Taleb
On the limits of planning, and the danger of mistaking a quiet stretch for a stable one.
When have you mistaken confidence — yours or someone senior to you — for actual competence?
What assumptions underpin your current plans — for your career, your division, your ship — that you haven't actually stress-tested?

What's Within Your Control

Perspective, at the end of it.
Not started
Meditations
Marcus Aurelius
Notes a man in command wrote only for himself. What is genuinely within your control, and what would a good officer look like if nobody noticed?
The Second Mountain
David Brooks
On the difference between the mountain you climb for yourself and the one you climb in service of something larger.

Is command the goal, or a vehicle for something else? What does this career mean if you never screen for it?

Phase VI — The Operating Manual
Bringing it together

Your Leadership Operating Manual

The living document. Revisit this every time you change billets, rank, or command.
Not started
Personal Mission Statement — in two or three sentences, why you serve and what you stand for.
Leadership Philosophy — how you intend to lead the people under you.
Command Climate Vision — what you want people to know happens the moment they walk into your division, department, or wardroom. What would never be tolerated?
Decision-Making Principles — the guardrails you want in place before you're tested, not after.
The hard question: at the end of this career, what would make you say "it was worth it" — and what would make you say it wasn't?
How to actually use this going forward
This page doesn't expire when you commission, qualify, or screen for command. The intent is to come back and revise it:
  • Re-read your Phase I and II answers every time you change duty stations.
  • Rewrite your Operating Manual before you take charge of anyone for the first time — division, department, or ship.
  • Export a copy at each milestone so you can see how your answers actually changed, not just how you remember them changing.
